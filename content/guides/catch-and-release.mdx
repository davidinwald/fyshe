# The Complete Guide to Catch and Release

Catch and release fishing is essential for maintaining healthy fish populations, especially in heavily pressured waters. When done correctly, released fish have survival rates above 95%.

## Before You Fish

### Use the Right Gear

- **Barbless hooks**: Crimp barbs with pliers or buy barbless hooks. They're easier to remove and cause less damage.
- **Appropriate tackle**: Use gear heavy enough to land fish quickly. Playing a fish to exhaustion on ultralight tackle significantly reduces survival.
- **Rubber nets**: Rubber mesh nets remove far less protective slime than nylon or cotton nets.

## During the Fight

- **Land fish quickly**: The longer the fight, the more lactic acid builds up in the fish's muscles. Use the heaviest tippet the situation allows.
- **Keep tension**: A steady, firm pressure tires the fish faster than stop-and-go fighting.
- **Avoid high water temperatures**: Fish are already stressed in warm water (above 68F / 20C for trout). Consider not fishing or switching to early morning hours.

## Handling the Fish

### The Golden Rules

1. **Wet your hands** before touching any fish. Dry hands strip protective slime.
2. **Keep the fish in the water** as much as possible. Every second out of water is like holding your breath underwater.
3. **Never squeeze** the fish's body. Support it gently with both hands.
4. **Avoid the gills**. The gills are extremely delicate and bleed easily.

### Removing the Hook

- Use hemostats or forceps to back the hook out the way it went in
- If the fish is deeply hooked, cut the leader close to the hook. The hook will dissolve or be expelled naturally
- Never pull or rip a hook from deep tissue

### For Photos

- Keep the fish in the water or hold it out for no more than 5 seconds
- Have your camera ready BEFORE lifting the fish
- Support the fish horizontally — never hold it vertically by the jaw (especially bass over 3 lbs)
- A fish in the water makes a great photo too

## The Release

1. **Face the fish upstream** in gentle current
2. **Support it upright** — one hand under the belly, one near the tail
3. **Wait for the fish to kick away** on its own
4. **Never throw** a fish back into the water

### Reviving a Tired Fish

If the fish doesn't swim away immediately:

- Hold it in gentle current facing upstream
- Move it in a slow figure-8 to pass water over its gills
- Wait patiently — it may take several minutes for a heavily fought fish to recover
- The fish is ready when it kicks strongly out of your hand

## Water Temperature Matters

Trout and other coldwater species are especially vulnerable in warm water:

| Water Temp | Risk Level | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| Below 60F (15C) | Low | Normal fishing |
| 60-65F (15-18C) | Moderate | Land fish quickly |
| 65-68F (18-20C) | High | Consider stopping |
| Above 68F (20C) | Critical | Do not fish for trout |

## Why It Matters

A single female trout can produce thousands of eggs over her lifetime. By releasing fish properly, you're investing in the future of the fishery and ensuring great fishing for generations to come.
